# Topic: Visualizing Embeddings (t-SNE, PCA)

## 1) Formal definition (what is it, and how can we use it?)

Visualizing word embeddings, such as those generated by Word2Vec, GloVe, or transformers, helps us understand the semantic relationships captured by these models. Word embeddings represent words as high-dimensional vectors, where the proximity of vectors in the embedding space reflects the semantic similarity of the words. However, high-dimensional spaces (e.g., 100, 300 dimensions) are difficult to visualize directly. Dimension reduction techniques like t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) and Principal Component Analysis (PCA) are used to project these high-dimensional embeddings into lower-dimensional spaces (typically 2D or 3D) that can be visualized on a scatter plot.

*   **t-SNE (t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding):** A non-linear dimensionality reduction technique that focuses on preserving the local structure of the high-dimensional data. It converts the high-dimensional Euclidean distances between data points into conditional probabilities that represent similarities. It then tries to minimize the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the joint probability distributions in the high-dimensional space and the low-dimensional space. This makes it excellent for revealing clusters and relationships within data. Due to its focus on local structure, global distances may not be accurately preserved. Its non-linear nature often leads to more visually distinct clusters than PCA.

*   **PCA (Principal Component Analysis):** A linear dimensionality reduction technique that identifies the principal components (orthogonal directions) that explain the maximum variance in the data. It projects the data onto a lower-dimensional subspace defined by these principal components. PCA is computationally faster than t-SNE and preserves global structure better. It is useful for understanding the overall variance and identifying the most important features in the data, but it may not always reveal complex relationships or distinct clusters as effectively as t-SNE, especially with non-linear data.

We use these visualizations to:

*   **Evaluate Embedding Quality:** Judge if semantically similar words are clustered together, indicating a good embedding.
*   **Identify Relationships:** Discover previously unknown relationships between words.
*   **Debug Models:**  Find words that are misplaced or have unusual relationships, potentially indicating issues in the embedding training process or dataset.
*   **Present Results:**  Communicate the findings of embedding-based analysis to a broader audience who may not be familiar with the underlying mathematics.

## 2) Application scenario

Imagine you have trained a Word2Vec model on a large corpus of text, and you want to understand how the model has learned to represent different words. You can use t-SNE or PCA to visualize the word embeddings in 2D.

For example, you might expect to see clusters of words related to:

*   **Countries/Cities:** "France", "Germany", "Paris", "Berlin"
*   **Animals:** "Dog", "Cat", "Lion", "Tiger"
*   **Sentiment:** "Happy", "Sad", "Angry", "Joyful"

By visualizing these embeddings, you can quickly see if your model has learned these relationships correctly. If "Dog" and "Cat" are close together in the visualization, it suggests the model has successfully captured their semantic similarity. If "France" is closer to "Dog" than to "Germany", this might indicate a problem with your training data or model parameters. If your corpus has articles discussing dogs in France frequently, then it may make sense why they are closer, revealing information about the corpus itself.

You can also visualize embeddings for documents, paragraphs or sentences as well, allowing to analyze topic clusters and document relationships.

## 3) Python method (if possible)

```python
import numpy as np
from sklearn.manifold import TSNE
from sklearn.decomposition import PCA
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Sample word embeddings (replace with your actual embeddings)
embeddings = {
    "king": np.array([4, 1, 2]),
    "queen": np.array([3, 2, 1]),
    "man": np.array([2, 1, 3]),
    "woman": np.array([1, 2, 4]),
    "apple": np.array([0, 5, 0]),
    "banana": np.array([0, 4, 1])
}

words = list(embeddings.keys())
embedding_matrix = np.array(list(embeddings.values()))

# --- t-SNE ---
tsne = TSNE(n_components=2, random_state=0, perplexity=2)
embeddings_2d_tsne = tsne.fit_transform(embedding_matrix)


plt.figure(figsize=(8, 6))
for i, word in enumerate(words):
    x, y = embeddings_2d_tsne[i, :]
    plt.scatter(x, y)
    plt.annotate(word, xy=(x, y), xytext=(5, 2), textcoords='offset points')
plt.title("t-SNE Visualization")
plt.show()


# --- PCA ---
pca = PCA(n_components=2)
embeddings_2d_pca = pca.fit_transform(embedding_matrix)

plt.figure(figsize=(8, 6))
for i, word in enumerate(words):
    x, y = embeddings_2d_pca[i, :]
    plt.scatter(x, y)
    plt.annotate(word, xy=(x, y), xytext=(5, 2), textcoords='offset points')
plt.title("PCA Visualization")
plt.show()
```
